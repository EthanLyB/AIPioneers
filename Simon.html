Herbert Simon

A Pioneer of AI and Decision Science

Herbert A. Simon (1916-2001) was an American political scientist, economist, sociologist, and psychologist whose work transformed multiple fields. He is considered one of the founding fathers of artificial intelligence, cognitive psychology, and organizational theory.

Early Life & Major Contributions

Born in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, Simon attended the University of Chicago, where his interest in the science of decision-making took root. His most famous contribution outside of AI is the concept of "bounded rationality."

This theory challenged the traditional economic view of perfectly rational decision-making, arguing that human decisions are limited by available time, information, and cognitive capacity. Instead of finding the perfect solution, people "satisfice" by choosing the first "good enough" option.

Pioneering Artificial Intelligence

Simon, along with Allen Newell and J.C. Shaw, developed what is considered the first artificial intelligence program, the Logic Theorist (1956). The program was designed to mimic human problem-solving skills and was able to prove theorems from Alfred North Whitehead and Bertrand Russell's Principia Mathematica.

They followed this with the General Problem Solver (GPS) in 1957, which was a more ambitious project aimed at creating a universal problem-solving machine using "means-ends analysis" to reduce the difference between a current state and a goal state.

Influence on Modern AI

Herbert Simon's work laid the theoretical groundwork for modern AI by focusing on the simulation of human thought processes. His research established the idea of a machine capable of symbolic reasoning and problem-solving, which is a core concept in much of today's AI. His insights into how humans make decisions continue to influence the design of AI systems.
